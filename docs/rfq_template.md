# RFQ Evaluation Methodology

This document explains the Request for Quotation (RFQ) scoring method used in the workbook. An RFQ is how a buyer compares competing suppliers fairly before awarding business.

## The problem an RFQ solves

When more than one supplier can provide the same material, price alone is a poor way to choose. The cheapest supplier might have the longest lead time, the worst payment terms, or the weakest quality record. An RFQ scoring matrix forces every supplier to be judged on the same criteria, weighted by what actually matters to the business.

## The criteria and weights

The workbook evaluates three competing suppliers for one material across five criteria. Each criterion carries a weight reflecting its importance to the business.

| Criterion | Weight | Why it carries this weight |
|---|---|---|
| Unit price | 40 percent | Direct cost impact, the largest single driver |
| Lead time | 20 percent | Shorter lead time reduces stock holding and stockout risk |
| Payment terms | 15 percent | Longer terms improve working capital |
| Quality rating | 15 percent | Defects create rework, returns, and customer risk |
| Local versus imported | 10 percent | Local reduces freight risk and supports resilience |

The weights sum to 100 percent. They are a business judgement, not a fixed rule. A business under cash pressure might weight payment terms higher. A business burned by quality issues might weight quality higher. The point of making the weights explicit is that the decision is transparent and defensible.

## How the scoring works

1. Each supplier is scored on each criterion.
2. Each score is multiplied by the criterion weight.
3. The weighted scores are summed into a total for each supplier.
4. The supplier with the highest weighted total is recommended.
5. A justification note records why, so the decision can be explained later.

XLOOKUP formulas pull each supplier's details automatically, so the matrix updates if the underlying supplier data changes.

## Why this matters to an employer

A purchasing officer who can run a structured RFQ is a purchasing officer who can defend a sourcing decision to a manager, an auditor, or a supplier who lost the business. It shows the buyer chose on value, not just price, and can prove it.
